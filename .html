Welcome to Atkaaq

Explore Canada's newest province, located between northern Manitoba and western Ontario, extending along the western shores of Hudson Bay. Atkaaq, meaning "Clear Land," is a province built on principles of sustainability, Indigenous governance, and harmony with nature.

About Atkaaq

Location & Geography

Location: Eastern edge of Manitoba to western Ontario, along the western shores of Hudson Bay

Landform Regions: Crosses the Canadian Shield and Hudson Bay Lowlands

Ecozones: Boreal Shield (south) and Hudson Plains (north)

Physical Geography:

  • Over 50,000 lakes and wetlands
  • Dominated by taiga (coniferous forests), muskeg, rocky outcrops, and permafrost areas
  • Mountainous ridges to the south formed by ancient volcanic activity, worn down by erosion
  • Glaciation during the last Ice Age created the thin soils and rugged landscape

Indigenous governance is embedded at every level ‚Äî a co-leadership model includes Elders, hereditary chiefs, and elected officials

Natural Resources & Sustainable Industries

1. Freshwater Systems

  • Major river systems: Asinabka River, Kitchi-Gami Lakes, and underground aquifers
  • Used for: drinking water, hydroelectricity, fish farming
  • Water is considered a "living relative" in Indigenous teachings ‚Äî protected under a provincial Water Accord signed with local nations

2. Forestry and Wild Foods

  • Timber and birch products are sustainably harvested
  • Wild rice, blueberries, and medicinal plants (e.g., sweetgrass) are also important economic goods
  • All resource zones follow a 7-generation plan: thinking long-term about impacts

Sustainable Industries

  • Eco-Tourism: guided canoe routes, aurora lodges, and Indigenous cultural camps
  • Tech-Coops: Northern solar panel manufacturing with youth apprenticeships
  • Forest Schools: Education programs for children held outdoors, in connection with the land

Population & Community Design

Demographics

  • Population: ~800,000
  • 35% Indigenous, 40% newcomers/immigrants, 25% Canadian-born settlers
  • Official Languages: English, French, Cree, and Ojibwe
  • Programs support language revitalization in schools and public signage

Minoaski ‚Äì Capital City

  • Planned with the concept of "Land Back + Future Forward"
  • Mixed-use districts reduce the need for long commutes
  • Mobility Hubs connect electric buses, snow trains, and lake ferries
  • Buildings are designed for cold climates: triple-insulated walls, heated floors powered by solar-thermal systems

Land Use Map Highlights:

  • 30% Conservation Areas (sacred sites, old-growth forests, and protected wetlands)
  • 25% Residential Zones (eco-villages, Indigenous housing clusters)
  • 20% Economic Zones (artisan markets, renewable energy tech parks)
  • 15% Transit/Infrastructure
  • 10% Community Services (schools, hospitals, learning circles)

Shared Governance:

  • The Atkaaq Land Council includes Indigenous Nations, settlers, and youth advisors
  • Policies must be agreed upon by both the provincial assembly and Elders' Circle

Sustainability Plan

Transportation:

  • No private gas vehicles
  • Free public transit powered by solar, wind, and hydro energy
  • Drones used for remote delivery in isolated winter regions
  • "Green Trails" ‚Äî bike paths that connect all major districts and include solar street lights

Energy System:

  • 80% Hydroelectric (low-impact dams with fish ladders)
  • 15% Solar: rooftop panels and solar farms in open areas
  • 5% Wind: designed to avoid migratory bird routes
  • Smart Grids and Battery Banks store extra energy for winter and emergencies

Housing & Architecture:

  • Passive Solar Design: large south-facing windows, natural materials, living roofs
  • Modular longhouses in Indigenous areas blend tradition and efficiency
  • Multi-generational housing models promote care-sharing and cultural continuity

Water & Food Systems:

  • Water protected through Indigenous Water Guardians programs
  • Urban farms + forest gardens produce year-round food using permaculture
  • Schools and homes have "Food Forest" patches ‚Äî kids grow local crops with Elders
